Investigating the relationship between rates of limiting long-term illness and proximity to motorways

In this project, I investigated whether rates of limiting long-term illness varied between census wards in Merseyside based on the proximity to moroways and data from the 2001 census.

To achieve this, I created a 5km buffer of the motorways running through Merseyside and then utilised a simple area-based interpolation to estimate the populations within and outside the buffer.

One issue with this methodology is that it assumes a homogenous distribution of population within each census ward which is unlikely to be the case. This assignment did not require a more sophisticated interpolation technique, however for my dissertation project I utilised binary dasymetric interpolation to address a similar problem. More information on that project will be added to this website shortly.

There was found to be a small 1% increase in the proportion of the population suffering from a LLTI within the motorway buffer when compared to those people living outside of the buffer. No statistical significance testing was required within the scope of the assessment and with such a small difference no definitive conclusion was reached.
